✅ CONFIRMED ACCURATE
Bitcoin 2021 ATH label — mostly fine. The chart labels the 2021 peak as "₿ PEAK 2021" at roughly $120k on the right axis scale. This is confusingly misleading. Bitcoin's actual 2021 ATH was $69,044 on November 10, 2021. However, looking carefully, the chart's line peaks at around $100k on the right axis in 2024 — not 2021. The "PEAK 2021" annotation appears to reference the axis tick at $120k, not claiming Bitcoin hit $120k in 2021. This is poor design but technically the label is pointing at a peak on the curve around the 2024 area.
Bitcoin post-2024 trajectory is broadly plausible. Bitcoin reached $125,835 as its all-time high on October 6, 2025. The chart shows a peak around $100k in the 2024–2025 range, which is consistent with reality.
Ukraine aid peaking in 2023 — roughly supported. Total aid to Ukraine amounted to approximately EUR 267 billion as of December 2024, or about EUR 80 billion per year. A peak in 2023 is plausible, as the Kiel data shows a sharp drop in new commitments in late 2023.

🚨 SIGNIFICANT PROBLEMS
1. The "₿ PEAK 2021" label is misleading/wrong
Bitcoin's closing price on December 31, 2021 was $46,328.94 — not anywhere near the $120k scale the annotation sits next to. The true 2021 ATH was ~$69k intraday. The chart's curve at the 2021 bar reaches only roughly $40–50k, which is consistent — but the annotation placement falsely implies the 2021 peak was much higher than it was.
2. The bar heights for Ukraine aid appear inflated
The chart shows the 2022 bar at ~$90B and 2023 bar at ~$105B. But the Kiel Institute's verified figure for total aid from Jan 2022 through end of 2024 was $119.6 billion — that's three full years combined. Single-year bars of $90B+ and $105B would be impossible under Kiel data. This is a major discrepancy. The chart appears to be using committed aid figures (which can be much larger than allocated/delivered aid), but even then, global aid commitments reached 400 billion euros total as of December 2024 — spread over three years, not concentrated in one. The per-year bars look roughly double what the data supports.
3. Pre-2022 Ukraine aid bars are misleading
The chart shows small blue bars going all the way back to 2010, framing them as comparable "international aid to Ukraine." The Kiel Institute tracker only covers aid since January 24, 2022. Pre-2022 figures (shown as IMF/development flows in the footnote) are a completely different category from war-related aid and mixing them on the same axis is an apples-to-oranges comparison designed to make the post-2022 surge look more dramatic.
4. "UA AID PEAK 2023" is questionable
New aid commitments actually dropped to their lowest level between August–October 2023 — an 87% drop vs. the same period in 2022. If peak delivered/allocated aid was 2023, that contradicts the Kiel tracker's own narrative that 2022 was the big surge year and 2023 saw a significant slowdown.
5. The 2026 Ukraine bar is speculative
Annual aid allocations averaged roughly EUR 41.6 billion in 2022–2024, and only EUR 32.5 billion had been allocated so far in 2025, with the tracker warning 2025 could be the lowest year since 2022. A 2026 bar showing ~$55B is not supported by available data and appears to be a fabricated projection presented without clear disclosure.

⚠️ FRAMING/DESIGN CONCERNS

Dual axis manipulation: By using two very different scales (left: billions USD for aid; right: price in USD for Bitcoin), the chart is designed to make the two trends look visually correlated. There is no established causal or even meaningful correlation between Bitcoin price and Ukraine aid — this is a classic misleading dual-axis chart trick.
Sources are real but selectively applied: CoinMarketCap, Kiel Institute, OECD, IMF, CFR are all legitimate sources — but the actual numbers shown don't match what those sources report.
The footnote disclaims "2025–26 partial" — burying the uncertainty for the most recent bars where the chart makes the boldest-looking claims.


Verdict
The chart is significantly misleading. The Bitcoin price curve is broadly accurate in shape, but the Ukraine aid bar magnitudes appear inflated by 2–3x versus the most reliable (allocated, not just committed) figures. The pre-2022 aid bars are an entirely different category mislabeled to appear continuous. The visual design implies a meaningful correlation between Bitcoin and Ukraine aid that isn't supported by data. Several key data points are either wrong, cherry-picked, or projections presented as facts.
